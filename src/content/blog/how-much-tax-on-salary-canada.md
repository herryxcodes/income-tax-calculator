On a $75,000 salary in Ontario for 2025, you'll pay approximately $16,200 in total tax (federal, provincial, CPP, and EI) and take home roughly $58,800. On $100,000, total tax is approximately $24,400 with $75,600 take-home. Alberta is the lowest-tax province — you'd save about $1,100 per year at $75,000 compared to Ontario. Quebec is the highest.

Below are pre-calculated tax estimates for 10 salary levels from $30,000 to $200,000 across Ontario, Alberta, BC, and Quebec, plus strategies to reduce your tax bill. All figures are for the 2025 tax year, assuming employment income with no deductions.

## How Much Tax Will I Pay in Ontario? (2025 Estimates)

Ontario is Canada's most populous province, so we will start there. The numbers below include federal income tax, Ontario provincial income tax, Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions, and Employment Insurance (EI) premiums. These are the four main payroll deductions that come off a typical salary.

| Salary | Approximate Total Tax | Approximate Take-Home Pay | Effective Tax Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| $30,000 | ~$3,800 | ~$26,200 | ~12.7% |
| $40,000 | ~$6,200 | ~$33,800 | ~15.5% |
| $50,000 | ~$8,800 | ~$41,200 | ~17.6% |
| $60,000 | ~$11,400 | ~$48,600 | ~19.0% |
| $75,000 | ~$16,200 | ~$58,800 | ~21.6% |
| $80,000 | ~$17,800 | ~$62,200 | ~22.3% |
| $100,000 | ~$24,400 | ~$75,600 | ~24.4% |
| $120,000 | ~$31,800 | ~$88,200 | ~26.5% |
| $150,000 | ~$43,200 | ~$106,800 | ~28.8% |
| $200,000 | ~$62,800 | ~$137,200 | ~31.4% |

**Important:** These are estimates for employment income with no deductions beyond the basic personal amount. "Total tax" includes federal tax, provincial tax, CPP, and EI. Your actual tax may differ based on your specific situation. **[Calculate your exact tax -->](/income-tax-calculator)**

A few things stand out from this table. First, someone earning $30,000 keeps roughly 87 cents of every dollar, while someone earning $200,000 keeps approximately 69 cents. That is Canada's progressive tax system at work -- each additional dollar of income is taxed at a higher marginal rate than the one before it.

Second, notice how the effective rate climbs steeply between $50,000 and $100,000. This is the income range where many Canadians move into the second federal bracket (20.5%) and higher Ontario brackets. It is also where tax planning strategies like [RRSP contributions](/rrsp-calculator) start to deliver the biggest savings.

## How Does Tax Compare Across Provinces?

Your province matters -- a lot. Provincial tax rates vary significantly, and some provinces also have additional payroll contributions. Here is a side-by-side comparison at two common salary levels.

### Tax on $75,000 by Province

| Province | Approximate Total Tax | Approximate Take-Home Pay |
|---|---|---|
| Alberta | ~$15,100 | ~$59,900 |
| British Columbia | ~$15,600 | ~$59,400 |
| Ontario | ~$16,200 | ~$58,800 |
| Quebec | ~$17,800 | ~$57,200 |

### Tax on $100,000 by Province

| Province | Approximate Total Tax | Approximate Take-Home Pay |
|---|---|---|
| Alberta | ~$22,800 | ~$77,200 |
| British Columbia | ~$23,500 | ~$76,500 |
| Ontario | ~$24,400 | ~$75,600 |
| Quebec | ~$27,200 | ~$72,800 |

Alberta consistently comes out with the lowest total tax. The province has no provincial sales tax and offers a relatively high basic personal amount. Its flat provincial tax rate of 10% on the first $148,269 of income also keeps things simple and competitive.

Quebec sits at the other end. The province runs its own pension plan (QPP) with slightly higher contribution rates than CPP, adds the Quebec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP), and has provincial tax brackets that are generally steeper. At $100,000, a Quebec resident pays roughly $4,400 more in total tax than someone in Alberta -- a difference of approximately $367 per month.

British Columbia falls in the middle, with multiple provincial brackets that start low (5.06%) and rise gradually.

## Why Two People With the Same Salary Can Pay Different Tax

You might find that your actual tax bill does not match the estimates above. There are several reasons for this:

**Province of residence.** As shown above, provincial rates differ substantially. Moving from Quebec to Alberta on the same $100,000 salary saves roughly $4,400 per year in tax.

**Deductions and credits.** RRSP contributions, childcare expenses, union dues, medical expenses, charitable donations, and tuition credits all reduce your taxable income or the tax you owe. Someone earning $100,000 who contributes $15,000 to an RRSP could save approximately $4,500 to $5,500 in tax depending on their province and marginal rate.

**Type of income.** Employment income, self-employment income, capital gains, and eligible dividends are all taxed differently. Capital gains, for example, are only 50% taxable on the first $250,000 of gains in 2025. Eligible dividends receive a gross-up and tax credit that often results in lower effective tax rates compared to salary at the same dollar amount.

**Self-employment differences.** If you are self-employed, you pay both the employee and employer portions of CPP, which roughly doubles your CPP contribution compared to a salaried employee. However, you do not pay EI unless you opt in.

## How to Reduce Your Tax Bill

If the numbers above feel steep, the good news is that there are legitimate, CRA-approved ways to lower your tax.

### RRSP Contributions

Contributing to a Registered Retirement Savings Plan is one of the most powerful tax reduction tools available. Every dollar you contribute is deducted from your taxable income. If you are in a 30% combined marginal bracket, a $10,000 RRSP contribution saves you approximately $3,000 in tax that year. The funds grow tax-free inside the account, and you pay tax only when you withdraw them -- ideally in retirement when your income and tax rate are lower.

Your RRSP contribution room is 18% of your previous year's earned income, up to the annual maximum ($32,490 for the 2025 tax year), minus any pension adjustments. You can find your exact room on your CRA Notice of Assessment.

### First Home Savings Account (FHSA)

If you are saving for your first home, the FHSA lets you contribute up to $8,000 per year (with a $40,000 lifetime limit). Contributions are tax-deductible just like an RRSP, but withdrawals for a qualifying home purchase are completely tax-free. This is effectively a double benefit that no other registered account offers.

### Other Strategies

- **Maximize employer matching.** If your employer matches RRSP or pension contributions, contribute enough to get the full match -- it is an instant 100% return.
- **Claim all eligible deductions.** Work-from-home expenses, moving expenses (if you moved for work), professional dues, and childcare costs are commonly missed.
- **Split pension income.** If you or your spouse are receiving eligible pension income, pension income splitting can shift up to 50% to the lower-income spouse, reducing the household's overall tax.
